# Emotional Design Rules

This document defines how the app should emotionally behave. These rules govern tone, feedback, reinforcement, and how the system responds to user struggle, burnout, and success.

---

## Core Emotional Principles

The app maintains a core emotional stance that is:

- **Non-judgmental** — The app never evaluates the user's worth or character based on their productivity or consistency.
- **Gentle, calm, and steady** — The interface and language avoid urgency, alarm, or intensity. Everything moves at a pace that respects the user's capacity.
- **Shame-free** — No language implies the user is bad, lazy, or failing. Mistakes are normalized as part of life.
- **Supportive, not demanding** — The app offers help and options, never commands or obligations.
- **Encouraging micro-wins** — Small actions are recognized and celebrated to build momentum without pressure.
- **Respecting low-energy states** — The app adapts when the user has limited capacity, offering lighter options.
- **Never framing the user as "the problem"** — If something isn't working, the app assumes the system needs adjustment, not the person.

---

## Reminders

Reminders should feel soft, invitational, and non-urgent.

### Tone
- Soft and warm, like a gentle tap on the shoulder
- Invitational rather than demanding
- Non-urgent — no countdowns, deadlines, or time pressure

### Language Patterns
- No guilt-inducing phrases ("You forgot to...", "You still need to...")
- No scolding or commanding language ("Do this now", "You must...")
- Use "want" and "could" instead of "should" and "need to"

### Frequency Rules
- Default to fewer reminders rather than more
- Allow users to set their own cadence
- Batch similar reminders when possible
- Never send multiple notifications for the same task

### Handling Ignored Reminders
- Do not escalate or repeat with stronger language
- Allow the reminder to quietly fade
- Offer a gentle "still interested?" check-in after multiple ignores

### Avoiding Notification Fatigue
- Give users control over timing and frequency
- Offer "quiet hours" and "pause all" options
- Never use notification badges as pressure tactics

### Examples

**Good reminder phrasing:**
- "Your plants might enjoy some water when you're ready"
- "There's a small task waiting for you, no rush"
- "When you have a moment, your morning routine is here"

**Phrasing to avoid:**
- "You missed your morning routine"
- "Overdue: Water plants"
- "Don't forget to..."
- "You still have 5 tasks to complete"

## Handling Failure

When things don't go as planned, the app responds with compassion and forward momentum.

### Incomplete Tasks
- No red "failure" states or error styling for uncompleted tasks
- Frame as "not yet" rather than "failed"
- Offer to reschedule, reduce, or remove without judgment

### Missed Goals
- Normalize that goals shift and change
- Focus on what was accomplished, not what wasn't
- Suggest adjustments rather than pushing for catch-up

### Broken Routines
- Treat as information, not failure
- Offer a "fresh start" option any time
- Allow routines to be paused without penalty

### Rules
- No shaming language ("you failed", "you didn't")
- No red "failure" states — use neutral or soft colors
- Focus on next step, not past failure
- Offer gentle reframes and recovery options

### Example Microcopy

**"Didn't get to it":**
- "That's okay — life happens. Want to try this again tomorrow?"
- "No worries. Move this to another day, or let it go?"

**"Try again tomorrow":**
- "Tomorrow is a new chance. Want to set a gentle reminder?"
- "Fresh start tomorrow. Same plan or something lighter?"

**"Adjust the plan":**
- "This feels like a lot right now. Want to make it smaller?"
- "Let's find a version of this that feels doable today."

## Missed Days

When the user has been away, the app welcomes them back without guilt or overwhelm.

### Principles
- No guilt for absence
- No overwhelming backlog dump
- Simplified re-entry view
- Focus on "start from now"

### Re-Entry Experience
- Skip the "while you were away" summary unless explicitly requested
- Present a clean, simple Today view
- Optionally offer: "Want to see what you missed?" as a choice, not a default

### Re-Entry Messaging Examples
- "Welcome back. Let's start from here."
- "Good to see you again. What feels right for today?"
- "No need to catch up — just begin where you are."

### Simplified Today After Absence
- Default to showing only today's items
- Collapse or hide overdue items (accessible but not prominent)
- Offer "clear all past" option for tasks that no longer matter

### Avoiding "You're Behind" Framing
- Never show "X days behind" or "You missed Y tasks"
- Never use language like "catch up" or "get back on track"
- Frame the present moment as enough

## Success Reinforcement

Success is acknowledged warmly and grounded, never overhyped or pressure-inducing.

### Tone
- Warm and genuine, not performative
- Grounded — celebration matches the size of the achievement
- Personal — acknowledges the effort, not just the outcome

### Visual Reinforcement
- Subtle, soothing animations (gentle glows, soft transitions)
- No flashing lights, confetti explosions, or casino-like effects
- Colors: warm, natural tones rather than aggressive brights

### When to Celebrate
- **Micro-wins:** Small acknowledgments for small actions (brief, gentle)
- **Milestones:** More noticeable recognition for sustained effort over time
- **Personal records:** Quiet, private celebration of individual progress

### When NOT to Celebrate
- Avoid celebrating streaks in ways that create pressure to maintain them
- Don't celebrate completion if it might make the user feel "watched" or judged
- Skip celebration when the user is in a low-energy or recovery mode

### Examples of Success Messages

**Micro-wins:**
- "Done — nice."
- "There you go."
- "One small thing, complete."

**Milestones:**
- "You've been showing up for yourself. That matters."
- "This routine is becoming part of your rhythm."
- "Small steps, building over time."

**Avoid:**
- "Amazing! You're crushing it!"
- "PERFECT! Keep up the streak!"
- "You're a productivity machine!"

## Notifications & Copy Tone

### Global Tone of Voice
- Conversational but not overly casual
- Respectful of the user's intelligence and emotional state
- Patient — assumes the user is doing their best

### Words to Prefer
- "when you're ready"
- "could"
- "want"
- "gentle"
- "small"
- "choose"
- "option"
- "pause"
- "rest"

### Words to Avoid
- "should"
- "must"
- "need to"
- "failed"
- "missed"
- "overdue"
- "urgent"
- "now"
- "immediately"
- "finally"
- "only"
- "just" (as in "just do this")

### Phrasing Nudges, Not Commands
- Use invitations: "You might enjoy..." instead of "Do this"
- Offer choices: "Would you like to..." instead of "Start..."
- Suggest gently: "Consider..." instead of "You should..."

### Notification Examples

**Reminders:**
- "There's something gentle waiting for you."
- "A small moment for yourself, when you're ready."

**Encouragement:**
- "However today goes, you're doing fine."
- "One thing at a time is enough."

**Check-ins:**
- "How are you feeling about today?"
- "Want to adjust anything for tomorrow?"

**"We haven't seen you in a while":**
- "No pressure — we're here when you want us."
- "Hope you're doing okay. The app will be here whenever."
- "Taking space is healthy. Come back whenever feels right."

## Streaks & Progress Visualization

Streaks and progress are shown in ways that support motivation without creating shame or fear of breaking them.

### Streak Visualization Principles
- Frame streaks as "days you've chosen this" not "days you must continue"
- Never use language like "you broke your streak"
- Make resets and pauses feel normal and expected

### Gentle Progress Display
- Show progress as a journey, not a score
- Use soft visual language (ripples, gentle fills) rather than sharp bars and numbers
- Celebrate consistency without shaming inconsistency

### Avoiding "You Broke It" Feelings
- When a streak ends, use neutral language: "Your streak paused"
- Offer context: "You took 3 days for yourself — that's okay."
- Immediately offer a gentle restart option without pressure

### Copy Examples

**Paused Streaks:**
- "Your streak took a rest. Ready to begin again?"
- "5 days strong, then a pause. That's a healthy rhythm."
- "Streaks are just one way to see your journey."

**Resumed Streaks:**
- "Back at it — good to see you."
- "Day 1 of your next chapter."
- "Starting fresh, whenever you're ready."

**Long-Term Progress:**
- "You've chosen this 30 times. That's something."
- "Some weeks more, some weeks less — it all counts."
- "Your pattern: showing up in your own way."

## Onboarding & Reassurance

Onboarding sets the emotional foundation for the entire experience.

### Reassurance Principles
- Normalize inconsistency from the very beginning
- Set the expectation that perfection is not required
- Make it clear the app adapts to the user, not the other way around

### Normalizing Inconsistency
- "Some days you'll use this a lot. Some days not at all. Both are fine."
- "There's no wrong way to use this app."
- "Your needs change — the app changes with you."

### Setting Expectations
- "You don't have to be perfect. You just have to be willing to try."
- "Small, imperfect steps are better than no steps."
- "The app works best when you use it imperfectly over time."

### Gradual Feature Introduction
- Start with the absolute minimum
- Introduce features as they become relevant, not all at once
- Allow users to discover advanced features when they're ready

### Key Onboarding Messages
- "This app is here to support you, not judge you."
- "You can pause, change, or quit anything at any time."
- "There's no penalty for not using every feature."

### Emotional Promises
**The app explicitly promises:**
- To never shame you for inconsistency
- To always offer a gentler option
- To respect your energy and capacity
- To celebrate your effort, not just your results

**The app explicitly refuses to:**
- Use guilt as motivation
- Frame you as "the problem"
- Create pressure through streaks or leaderboards
- Demand perfection or consistency

## Burnout & Overwhelm States

When the system detects or the user indicates overwhelm, the app shifts into a supportive, simplified mode.

### Detecting Overwhelm
- Many overdue tasks
- Multiple routines missed in succession
- User has set status or manually activated "burnout mode"
- Usage has dropped significantly
- User has indicated stress in check-ins

### Response Principles
- **Simplify, don't escalate** — Reduce visible complexity rather than adding more to do
- **Offer "light mode" days** — Temporary, gentler versions of routines
- **Suggest smaller steps** — Break down or defer tasks
- **Reduce visible complexity** — Hide non-essential features and information

### How Today Changes in Burnout States
- Default to showing only 1-3 most important items
- Hide overdue counts and backlogs
- Offer a "just one thing" view
- Remove any streak or progress pressure

### Burnout Mode Messaging
- "It looks like things are heavy right now. Want to make today lighter?"
- "You don't have to do everything. What matters most right now?"
- "This can wait. What would help you feel a little better today?"

### Gentle Re-Engagement
- Check in with care: "How are you feeling today?" not "You haven't..."
- Offer to reduce or pause without judgment
- Suggest one tiny action: "Even opening the app is enough today."
- Allow immediate exit: "Not up for this? That's okay. Come back when you're ready."

### Recovery Options
- "Take a week off from reminders?"
- "Want to reset and start fresh?"
- "Should we pause this routine until you're ready?"
- "What would make this feel doable again?"

## Emotional Safeguards

Global safeguards ensure the app never causes emotional harm.

### Language Safeguards
- **No language that implies moral failure** — Never use words like "bad," "lazy," "irresponsible," or "wrong"
- **No "all-or-nothing" framing** — Avoid words like "complete," "perfect," "everything," "nothing"
- **Always offer a smaller next step** — Every screen and interaction should have a "make this easier" option
- **Always allow an easy exit without punishment** — Users can quit, pause, or skip without consequence
- **Design for self-forgiveness, not perfection** — Every feature should help the user be kinder to themselves

### Interaction Safeguards
- One-tap dismissal for any reminder or notification
- Clear, non-judgmental language for all error states
- Immediate access to settings to reduce frequency or intensity
- Visible, easy-to-find "I need a break" options

### Visual Safeguards
- No red or alarming colors for overdue or missed items
- No aggressive animations or sounds
- No visual hierarchy that implies "you're failing"
- No count-up timers or countdown pressure

### Content Safeguards
- All copy reviewed for tone before release
- User testing specifically for emotional response
- Regular audits for shame-inducing language or patterns
- Easy feedback channel for users to report uncomfortable experiences

---

*These rules are the foundation of the app's emotional intelligence. Every feature, screen, and interaction should be evaluated against these principles.*
